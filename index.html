Dr. Yifat Aran is a legal scholar whose work bridges corporate law, securities regulation, and the study of innovation ecosystems. She is an Assistant Professor at the University of Haifa Faculty of Law, a fellow at The Project on Israel and the Regional Economy at Harvard Business School, and a fellow at the Rutgers Institute for the Study of Employee Ownership and Profit Sharing. She also teaches in the MBA program at the Technion – Israel Institute of Technology. Her research focuses on the legal architecture of innovation ecosystems, with an emphasis on comparative venture law.

Dr. Aran earned her J.S.D. and J.S.M. from Stanford Law School, where she was a fellow of the Stanford Interdisciplinary Graduate Fellowship (SIGF) and the John M. Olin Program in Law and Economics. Her scholarship combines doctrinal analysis, empirical methods, and policy engagement, with recent work examining equity-based compensation, founder-centric governance, and venture capital market dynamics. Her work has been published in leading journals including the Stanford Law Review, the University of Illinois Law Review, and the Journal of Law, Economics, & Organization.

Through her work, Dr. Aran examines how private ordering structures ownership and control in startup firms, and how legal institutions reliably support financial capital, yet often fail the human capital on which innovation depends.

Publications

Law Reviews

Yifat Aran, Brian J. Broughman & Elizabeth Pollman, “Exit after Exit,” Harv. Bus. L. Rev. (forthcoming).

Challenges the conventional view that an IPO marks a clean exit for venture capital investors and a sharp transition from private to public governance. Drawing on evidence from venture-backed IPOs, the paper shows that venture capitalists often retain substantial post-IPO influence through continued ownership, voting rights, and board presence, such that the IPO functions as a staged and fluid governance transition rather than an endpoint. Reframes debates over dual-class stock and founder control by documenting persistent founder-VC coalitions and hybrid governance arrangements in public firms.

Forthcoming

Gad Weiss, Kobi Ben Chitrit & Yifat Aran, “Let it Go: A Comparative Perspective on the Enforceability of Pay-to-Play Provisions,” TAU Law Rev. (forthcoming).

Provides the first comparative analysis of pay-to-play provisions in venture capital across multiple jurisdictions, examining how different legal regimes shape investor protections and follow-on investment incentives.

Forthcoming

Yifat Aran & Nizan Geslevich Packin, “Due Diligence Dilemma,” 2025 U. Ill. L. Rev. 101 (2025).

Identifies a core tension in venture capital between the imperative to invest rapidly and the expectation that VCs serve as gatekeepers through independent diligence, showing how cyclical market conditions incentivize “proxy due diligence”—inferring trustworthiness from the presence of other reputable investors rather than conducting independent verification.

2025

Elizabeth Pollman & Yifat Aran, “Ousted,” 25 Theoretical Inquiries L. 231 (2024).

Challenges the prevailing assumption that founder-CEOs with voting control are untouchable, documenting numerous cases where founders were ousted despite retaining formal power, and mapping the countervailing forces—board dynamics, investor pressure, and reputational mechanisms—that can override governance protections.

2024

Yifat Aran, “The Start-up Law of the Start-up Nation,” 52 Heb. U. L. Rev. (Mishpatim) 115 (2025).

Maps the organizational features of venture-backed startups in Israel and analyzes Israeli court cases on shareholder disputes, advocating for a nuanced legal framework adapted to the unique governance dynamics of the startup ecosystem.

2025

Yifat Aran, “Making Disclosure Work for Start-Up Employees,” 2019 Colum. Bus. L. Rev. 867 (2019).

Proposes a regulatory framework to improve information disclosure to startup employees receiving equity compensation, arguing that current securities exemptions leave employees unable to assess the value and risks of their stock options.

2019

Yifat Aran, “Beyond Covenants Not to Compete: Equilibrium in High-Tech Startup Labor Markets,” 70 Stan. L. Rev. 1235 (2018).

Develops a novel theoretical framework showing how equity-based compensation serves as a functional substitute for noncompete agreements in startup labor markets, with implications for employee mobility, innovation policy, and the design of compensation contracts.

2018

Peer-Reviewed Journals

Yifat Aran, “Shared Capitalism at a Crossroads: Equity Compensation and Factor Bargaining Power in the Innovation Economy,” Int’l Rev. Applied Econ. 1–18 (2026).

Frames broad-based equity compensation in tech as a structural response to human-capital scarcity and high labor mobility, and argues that artificial intelligence may constitute a fundamental break in this arrangement. By eroding the scarcity that historically justified widespread equity grants, AI threatens to shift the balance of bargaining power between labor and capital. The article examines the institutional conditions under which shared capitalism might nonetheless persist.

2026

Yifat Aran & Raviv Murciano-Goroff, “Equity Illusions,” 41(1) The Journal of Law, Economics, and Organization 196 (2025).

Presents original experimental and survey evidence that startup employees harbor systematic “market illusions” about equity compensation, responding to economically irrelevant signals when deciding to forgo cash for stock options—pointing to significant labor market inefficiencies in the tech sector.

2025

Book Chapters

Yifat Aran, “The RSU Time Bomb: Regulating Startup Equity Compensation in the Unicorn Era,” in Research Handbook on the Structure of Private Equity and Venture Capital (Brian J. Broughman & Elisabeth de Fontenay eds., Edward Elgar Publ’g 2026).

Examines how the shift from stock options to restricted stock units (RSUs) in late-stage startups creates new risks for employees, particularly through double-trigger vesting structures, and proposes regulatory responses for the unicorn era.

2026

Yifat Aran & Moran Ofir, “The Effect of Specialised Courts over Time,” in Time, Law and Change: An Interdisciplinary Study (Sanne Taekema, Sofia Ranchordás & Yaniv Roznai eds., Hart Publ’g 2020).

Evaluates the impact of Israel’s specialized corporate court on corporate and securities law adjudication, providing empirical evidence on how judicial specialization evolves and affects case outcomes over time.

2020

Working Papers

Yifat Aran, Brian J. Broughman & Elizabeth Pollman, “CEO Turnover in Dual-Class Firms,” U. Pa. Inst. for L. & Econ. Rsch. Paper No. 24-38 (Dec. 2024).

Provides the first large-scale empirical study of CEO turnover at dual-class firms, revealing that founder-CEOs at companies with differential voting structures are replaced more frequently than commonly assumed, and analyzing the governance mechanisms that facilitate these transitions.
